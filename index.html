My Writing Style

I write psychological thrillers and love to get into your head. One tool I rely on is ambiguity. In my opinion, a monster that may exist but may not exist is much scarier than a monster that is entirely real. Another tool I use is something I like to call unreliable perception. Some psychological thrillers use unreliable narrators, I dislike that. Unreliable narration is the narrator lying to you whereas unreliable perception is the narrator not knowing what's the truth and what's a lie, which can get into your head much easier.

My Writing and Revision Process

I write rather fast for a pscyhological thriller but I find my instincts are much better than others'. I finished the first draft of my first psychological thriller in two and a half months. The editing process takes much longer however. Like many authors, I follow a simple revision process. Distance first, to let your brain forget where you wanted the story to go in the first place. Outlining next, going chapter by chapter, event by event. Then full rewriting, from scratch, I find this helps develop your voice and uniqueness. This entire process seemed to click for me like it has for many authors before.

About Me

I started writing three years ago, beginning with short fantasy and sci‑fi stories. Even though those early pieces weren’t perfect, they were essential — they taught me how to build worlds, shape characters, and experiment with ideas. They also remind me how far I’ve come.

My biggest inspirations come from mythology and classic literature like The Odyssey. I love weaving subtle mythic undertones into modern psychological stories. In my latest thriller, for example, I named the main character Lethe, after the Greek river of forgetting. Details like that let me blend ancient themes with contemporary tension.

Today, I focus on psychological thrillers and horror. I’m drawn to ambiguity, unreliable perception, and stories that make readers question what’s real. My background in myth and speculative fiction helps me create narratives that feel both timeless and unsettling — the kind of stories that stay in your head long after you finish them.

Writing Samples

Clinical, Explanatory Horror

Waking up is always supposed to be a relief. When a nightmare gets too loud or a dream gets too real, your bed is always there to comfort you. But what if waking up did the opposite? It shatters any type of comfort you have left. If a person lives a lie, and that lie is revealed to be one, it can break them. Truth can be too heavy to carry. One may try to rebuild the lie they lived, piece by piece. They can’t deal with the truth. They seek comfort in any place they can find it. They’ll go back under, back to sleep, before they woke up.

They can’t run away though. When a nightmare gets created by one's mind, they can’t outrun it. It takes up their thoughts and, by extension, their life. No matter what they do, the nightmare finds its way to the top of their mind. Like a worm, wriggling into whatever you didn’t want it to.

People don’t vanish. They get erased, and then overwritten. Then the person you once knew becomes a memory, wearing a mask of who they once were. People around them try to see them the same. They often think the old person they knew is still there. They aren’t. I want them back. They can’t come back. I often try to fill in the blank, see what isn’t there. It never can work. The person they knew is gone and they can’t bring them back.

The truth is obvious about who someone has become. The people around them don’t accept it. I make my own truth, deciding what’s happening. They spiral; if they can’t accept anything, they can’t live. They can survive, but not live.

1st Person Hallucination Scenes

I wake up in a hospital bed. The fluorescent lights above me buzz in uneven intervals—4 long, 6 short, 4 long, 4 long, 6 short. A nurse comes in and presses a syringe into my arm; the edges of my vision start to smear but refuse to fully darken. She switches the IV bag. The beeps of every machine in the room seem louder, then quieter. The room becomes still, no sound, no visual other than gray walls and tile floors.

My thoughts start to materialize in front of me. A mirror at the end of the room turns and contains my mother. She stares, not moving, just staring. She wriggles her way into my mind like a worm. Eli stands in the corner, repeatedly running his fingers along the wall in deliberate patterns. No—letters. He writes what he's thinking, always repeated. “Kill me, Kill me.” Then, “Why’d you do it, Why’d you do it?”

I try to scream, but nothing comes out. My muscles contract but don’t move me. Wait—no, I can move. I stand up and walk out. My instinct says to run, but my legs only move at a leisurely pace. I walk down the hallway. The crimson glow of the exit sign invites me, pulling my legs out of my control.

Suddenly Eli appears. Copies of him line the walls, tracing his messages. The letters appear red, deeper than the sign—blood. He writes, “YOU DID IT, YOU DID IT, YOU DID IT” over and over. I reach the exit and, with all my strength, push open the doors.

Dream Sequences

The Dreamer walks into the funeral home and sits in the waiting room. All the seats are packed with dark shadows of people, crowding around him. One, seeming to be the only one with life, stares at him, screaming, “YOU DID IT, YOU DID IT” over and over.

Every room he walks into, his mother’s corpse sits on a tray. Copies and copies of her. Every door he walks through, her body slides into the refrigerated shelf. Then he bursts through the final door. His mother suddenly sits up. “You did this. You did this to me.”

He bolts into the street, subconsciously counting every car he sees and its color: 1: red, 2: green, 3: gray, 4: white, 5: black, 6: black, 7: blue, 8: green, 9: gray, 10: red. The final red car—the last he counts—suddenly runs the Dreamer over.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How does ghostwriting work?
A: You come to me with an idea. I flesh it out in whatever format and length you want. We revise together as many times as you need. You own the story, and I get paid.

Q: What genres do you write?
A: I offer all types of psychological fiction and horror.

Q: How long will a project take?
A: It depends on the project type, but generally, including revisions: novellas take 4–6 months, short stories take 1–2 months, and flash fiction takes 0.5–1.5 months.

Q: What does the revision process look like?
A: I work with you on rewrites, specifying what you want to keep, change, or cut.

Q: How do I pay?
A: I accept Zelle and PayPal. Payment occurs before the drafting process begins.

Q: How will communication work?
A: I offer communication over email and Discord. Text-only is preferred. Updates happen every two weeks, and I respond within 48 hours.

Q: What do I need for voice matching?
A: Five writing samples, each 100–500 words, covering different topics.

Q: Do I own the story?
A: Yes, 100%. I get paid, and the rights are entirely transferred to you.

Q: How many revisions do I get?
A: As many as you need until you're satisfied.

Q: How do I start?
A: Send me a list of characters and their roles, a basic summary, your desired word count, the tone of the story, and any inspiration.

Q: Are there any content boundaries?
A: Yes. I do not write explicit or hateful content. I only write psychological fiction with light violence.
